1.0 Tools for the scientist

In order to describe a situation we need:

A consistent language: Names and symbols for different quantities.
A way of quantifying: Numbers (maths) and units.

To make our lives easier we also:

Reduce number of zeros: Standard form and unit prefixes
Use few digits in calculations: Rounding, significant figures and decimal places
Make simple estimates: Order of magnitude

The physical world is messy, we can calculate and express our Uncertanties

Erros in measurement: Absolute, fractional and percentage uncertainties
Errors on graphs: Error bars
Erros in our final result: Uncertainty of gradient and intercepts

As well as a size some quantities have a direction

Expressing quantities > 1D Vectors
Calculation in > 1D Combination and Resolving into components

1.1 Measurement in Physics

The terms: massive, weight, moment, battery and many others have specific meanings in physics.

Massive: An object/particle that has mass (some don't)
Weight: The force due to gravity (not measured in Kg)
Moment: The turning effect of a force
Battery: A group of ___ (e.g. electic cells) in series or parallel (like battery hens, artillery battery)

Using terminology correctly allows clear communication of ideas.

1.1.1 Quantities, Symbols and Units

There appear to be properties of the universe that we can measure and calculate.

Things such as distance, time, mass can be measured and relationships calculated.

We need a...

  • Name to call the quantity. (and symbol for use in calculations)
  • Common unit of measurement (and symbol for brevity)

The most basic (or easiest to measure) are the fundamental (base) quantities

Others that can be given in terms of the fundamental quantities are called derived quantities

e.g. Distance and Time are fundamental quantities with units meters and seconds

Speed is a derived quantity with unit meters per second

All terms in an expression must have the same units

Height = initial height + growth per year * number of years

1.1.3 Standard form

The length of the universe is about 1000000000000000000000000 meters and the radius of an atom is about 0.000000000000001 meters. When measuring length, scientists will usually use the metre (m). To describe very small or very large objects standard form is often used so that quantities can be easily compared.

An estimate (good guess) can allow an approximate answer to be calculated, this can be useful when testing a theory or result.

You may be asked to estimate to 2 sig fig, 1 sig fig or the nearest order of magnitude.

1.2 Uncertanties and errors

1.2.1 Accuracy of numbers

I could measure a bit of string and find that its length is 23.2cm...
What does 23.2 mean? How about 23.20?

It doesn’t just mean exactly 23.2, it actually expresses a range of possible values. 23.15 ≤ 23.2 < 23.25

Likewise 23.20 doesn’t mean exactly 23.2, it expresses the range 23.195 ≤ 23.20 < 23.205

The number of decimal places and/or significant figures can be used to express accuracy.

In ambiguous cases you should state the sig.fig/d.p after the value e.g.[100m (2 s.f.)] or use ±

Significant Figures

When doing calculations you should never give your answer to higher accuracy (sig-fig) than the question:

Decimal Places

When recording data from an instrument in general the same number of d.p should be used.
This is because most instruments are accurate to an absolute value.
e.g ruler (accurate to 0.5mm)

1.2.2 Errors, precision and accuracy

Random errors: Errors due to instrument, experimenter or environment which are not constant.
(e.g. temperature, wind, misreading, uncontrollable variables)

Systematic errors: Errors due to instrument, experimenter whichcause a consistent error.
(e.g. miscalibration, parallax)

Precision and accuracy

Accuracy: How close the results are to the ‘real’ result.

Precision: How close together the results are.

Random errors spread out results – low precision

Systematic errors move the average result - low accuracy

Random errors can be compensated for by _________.

Systematic errors can be detected in the graphical analysis.

1.2.3 Uncertanties

Inaccuracy in measurement

Reading the scale results in the most common inaccuracy.
The usual value is ± ½ of the smallest gradation.

Additional inaccuracies can be due to the method of measurement or additional factors.
